# What led me into it
Reading the Fountainhead, I was truly influenced by the ideology of the Fountainhead, seeing myself in some of its characters. I decided to try out Rand's magnum opus, which I heard dives deeper into her philosophy, Objectivism.

# What did I learn initially
Initially, I was confused by the enduring monologues, which covered abstract ideas   I was not familiar with. Cliffhangers that added complexity to already esoteric concepts were not friendly to me, a rather beginner reader. I was able to grasp the surface level concepts about the morality of production which stated that those who produce should not be hampered by those who rely on them. Deeper concepts like the sanction of the victim, though, went over my head until I reached the end of the book.

# What did I learn upon closely analyzing it
Analyzing this book for the [Atlas Shrugged](https://aynrand.org/students/essay-contests/atlas-shrugged/) essay contest granted me a deeper understanding. Abstract concepts that Francisco, Rearden, and Dagny all hint at in their dialogues became clear. The various character vs. character conflicts that were balanced with the character vs. society conflicts show their true light upon a second look. I decided to answer a question about the morality of production, something I immediately understood about the book, even after my initial read. This culminated in an [essay](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1PRk0A4SewS5KAgAavTif7s5GqdzeFPLY/view?usp=sharing) that I am proud of.

# Takeaways
Atlas Shrugged and Objectivism has had a large influence on my outlook on my life and our current world. Similarly to my experience reading Titan by Ron Chernow about Rockefeller, Atlas Shrugged has reinforced my idea that begging and undeserved alms are unrealistic. The future is under your own control.

Despite being a such a dense book, I can summarize my takeaways in three key points.
**1. Your mind is your greatest tool.**
Hank Rearden, Dagny Taggart, or any of the other industrialists/producers relies on their own convictions. They use their mind to move the earth. They understand that their mind is their only sole possession, and they treat it that way. 
**2. Nothing worthwhile is undeserved.**
Anything that has value was synthesized by a thinking mind. Someone somewhere put an effort into its production. The sacrifice of the power of their mind to create makes it worthwhile.

Francisco's speech drives this home with his praise of money and how it is a claim on another mind's time. Money entrusts its traders to get the most of it, driving productive competition that benefits everyone. 

One can broaden this transactional microcosm to all parts of life, including daily interactions. While this may not be healthy and practical, understanding that reward requires effort and you get what you put in (A is A) can help you live a more fulfilling life. 
**3. One's partner is a reflection of themselves.**
When Hank realizes that his desire for Dagny is not to be condemned, he has an epiphany. Instead of seeing this desire as carnal, he sees it as virtuous and merited. This is due to the fact that their relationship is a celebration of themselves and their minds, not a lecherous, wanton indulgence. 

It is worth noting that Rand continues to mention something along the lines of "a vacuous mind is not worth conquering." She ties relationships to the concept of trade by claiming that nothing is unearned, and therefore you should expect from a relationship what you put into it. In Rand's view, contribution mainly stems from the mind, and therefore partners are a reflection of your own mind.

# Wrap up
All in all, I would strongly recommend reading Atlas Shrugged and other books by Rand (including the Fountainhead, which I arguably liked more). They are wonderful books that give you a good kick of libertarian and while seeming polemic, actually conform to ancient wisdom in that they venerate hard work and effort like some other religions. 

Thanks for taking the time to read!
