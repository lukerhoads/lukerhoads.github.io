## Project Overview

In this project I designed and fabricated a carbon fiber windsurf/SUP board with an EPS plug with XPS reinforcements near the fin and mast box. I used two layers of 3k 6oz carbon fiber over the EPS plug, with the first layer oriented at a 45 degree angle to the visible layer. 

## Project Motivation

Swimming in Lake Michigan is fun, but why not try other activities like SUP / windsurf?

## Process

First, I got a rough idea of what I wanted in CAD. I used Fusion 360 to model out rough dimensions based on a reference windsurfing board.

![A Couple Lofts](/images/windsurf-board/CAD.png)

Then, I bought foam materials and planned out how to cut it. Using a hotwire was the most budget friendly option, so that is what I went with. 

![Glueing the Blank Together](/images/windsurf-board/IMG_1247.jpg)

Running to glue it together in time.

![Sanding and Wirecutting](/images/windsurf-board/IMG_1266.jpg)

I was able to get a rough shape of the board, which I sanded down.

I then layed up the board with carbon fiber, using typical supplies such as towels to act as a breather cloth, a polyethylene vacuum bag, and some nylon ripstop cloth for the peel ply. Looking back, using the nylon ripstop as a budget alternative to authentic peel ply wasn't the best idea because the ripstop does not absorb the resin as well. That said, butyl sealant tape is a true budget consumable replacement for gum tape!

Due to mistakes made when laying up, such as not putting boundary tape to prevent epoxy runout under the board, lots of manual cutting was required to prepare it for the bottom side layup. The same process was used.

I then cut out the holes for the fin and was able to get it out on the water. While it floats, I realized that it has the stability profile of a surfboard, not a SUP board. Maybe if Lake Michigan gets enough wind and I buy a sail I can try it out on the waves!

## Challenges

* During the carbon layup, creases in the carbon and a lack of tape on the underside of the board resulted in inconsistencies that needed to be post-processed, including sharp corners 
* In the process of making the hotwire, the power supply blew many times due to improper current regulation due to the Nichrome wire burning up. To address this a new power supply was purchased and used in 2 minute intervals to avoid stressing the power supply.
* Many imperfections (that still show) made preparation for the lamination difficult. In hindsight, a CNC router would've made the process much easier due to the imperfections that result from hand shaping the board.
* I attempted making a fin using infusion techniques but ran into problems with resin voids, an improper layup schedule, poor surface finish, and difficulties with equipment. The small size of the fins make infusion especially difficult to fit all of the equipment that supposedly help with resin distribution. While I was able to achieve relatively smooth 3D printed molds, I still believe that the surface finish on the molds negatively altered the final result, which was not strong enough. Instead, I opted for a pre-built plastic injection molded fin.

## Key Achievements

* Hand sculpting EPS foam blank using DIY hotwire cutter made with PVC pipe, Nichrome wire, and a switch.
* Vacuum-bag lamination of board with 3K carbon fiber in arrangement to promote stiffness
* Integrating US Mast Box Windsurfing Fin into foam blank with XPS reinforcement and epoxy adhesives
